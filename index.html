What Are Lewis Structures?

Lewis structures, also called Lewis dot diagrams or electron dot structures, are diagrams that show the bonding between atoms in a molecule and the lone pairs of electrons that may exist.

They were introduced by Gilbert N. Lewis in his 1916 paper "The Atom and the Molecule" and remain one of the most useful tools for understanding molecular structure.

Key Concept

Lewis structures show valence electrons as dots around atomic symbols. Bonding electrons are shown as lines between atoms, while non-bonding electrons (lone pairs) are shown as pairs of dots.

The Octet Rule

Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a full outer shell of 8 electrons (like the noble gases).

Exception: Hydrogen only needs 2 electrons (duet rule) to fill its outer shell.

After bonding

How to Draw Lewis Structures

Follow these five steps to draw the Lewis structure of any molecule.

1

Count Total Valence Electrons

Add up all valence electrons from each atom. For ions, add electrons for negative charges or subtract for positive charges.

Example: CO₂

Carbon: 4 e⁻ × 1 = 4

Oxygen: 6 e⁻ × 2 = 12

Total: 16 valence electrons

2

Identify the Central Atom

The central atom is usually the least electronegative element (excluding hydrogen). It's often the atom that can form the most bonds.

Common central atoms:

C, N, S, P (not H or halogens)

In CO₂: Carbon is central

3

Draw Single Bonds First

Connect each outer atom to the central atom with a single bond. Each bond uses 2 electrons.

O — C — O

Used: 4 electrons (2 bonds × 2 e⁻)

Remaining: 12 electrons

4

Complete Octets on Outer Atoms

Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs on outer atoms first, giving each atom 8 electrons (2 for hydrogen).

:O — C — O:

Each O gets 6 more e⁻ (3 lone pairs)

Used: 12 electrons

Remaining: 0 electrons

5

Form Multiple Bonds if Needed

If the central atom doesn't have an octet, convert lone pairs from outer atoms into bonding pairs (double or triple bonds).

Carbon only has 4 e⁻, needs 4 more!

Move lone pairs to form double bonds:

O = C = O

Now all atoms have octets!

Formal Charge

Formal charge helps determine the best Lewis structure when multiple structures are possible.

Formal Charge Formula

FC = V - L - B

FC = Formal Charge

V = Valence electrons (from periodic table)

L = Lone pair electrons (non-bonding)

B = Bonding electrons ÷ 2

Rules for Best Lewis Structure

  • The sum of all formal charges should equal the overall charge of the molecule/ion
  • Formal charges should be as close to zero as possible
  • Negative formal charges should be on more electronegative atoms
  • Like charges should not be on adjacent atoms

Example: CO₂ Formal Charges

0 O = 0 C = 0 O

Oxygen: FC = 6 - 4 - 2 = 0

Carbon: FC = 4 - 0 - 4 = 0

Sum of formal charges = 0 (neutral molecule)

Resonance Structures

Some molecules can be represented by multiple valid Lewis structures called resonance structures.

Resonance structures are different Lewis structures for the same molecule where electrons are arranged differently. The actual molecule is a hybrid of all resonance structures.

Important!

Resonance structures differ only in electron placement, NOT atom positions. The double-headed arrow (↔) connects resonance structures.

Example: Ozone (O₃)

↔

In reality, both O-O bonds in ozone are equivalent, with a bond order of 1.5 (between single and double).

Exceptions to the Octet Rule

While most atoms follow the octet rule, there are important exceptions.

Incomplete Octet

Some atoms are stable with fewer than 8 electrons.

BF₃ - Boron has only 6 electrons
BeH₂ - Beryllium has only 4 electrons

Expanded Octet

Elements in period 3+ can have more than 8 electrons using d orbitals.

SF₆ - Sulfur has 12 electrons
PCl₅ - Phosphorus has 10 electrons

Odd Electron Species

Molecules with an odd number of valence electrons cannot satisfy the octet rule for all atoms.

NO - 11 valence electrons
NO₂ - 17 valence electrons

Practice Problems

Test your understanding by drawing Lewis structures for these molecules.

1 H₂S Easy

Total valence electrons: 2(1) + 6 = 8

Structure: H—S—H with 2 lone pairs on sulfur

Explanation: Similar to water, sulfur is central with two bonds to hydrogen and two lone pairs.

2 CCl₄ Easy

Total valence electrons: 4 + 4(7) = 32

Structure: Carbon center with 4 single bonds to Cl atoms

Explanation: Each Cl has 3 lone pairs, carbon has no lone pairs.

3 SO₂ Medium

Total valence electrons: 6 + 2(6) = 18

Structure: O=S—O or O—S=O (resonance structures)

Explanation: Sulfur is central with one double bond, one single bond, and one lone pair. Has resonance!

4 CN⁻ Medium

Total valence electrons: 4 + 5 + 1 = 10

Structure: [:C≡N:]⁻

Explanation: Triple bond between C and N, each with one lone pair. The negative charge is on carbon.

5 NO₃⁻ Hard

Total valence electrons: 5 + 3(6) + 1 = 24

Structure: Nitrogen center with one double bond and two single bonds to oxygen

Explanation: Has 3 equivalent resonance structures. Each N-O bond has bond order of 1.33.

6 XeF₄ Hard

Total valence electrons: 8 + 4(7) = 36

Structure: Xe center with 4 single bonds to F and 2 lone pairs

Explanation: Expanded octet! Xenon has 12 electrons around it. Square planar geometry.
